Creating a new digital currency

07 September 2017
  | Source: Group
The Utility Settlement Coin could make it quicker, cheaper and less risky to clear and settle payments between banks.
Page Content

Niall Cameron: It could revolutionise clearing and settlement

We are one of 10 banks developing the USC, a new virtual currency built on blockchain technology.

The currency, which is expected to launch by the end of 2018, will enable financial institutions to settle trades and payments over a distributed ledger in seconds, instead of days – and at a lower cost and with less risk.

“USC has the potential to revolutionise the clearing and settlement process between institutions,” said Niall Cameron, our Global Head of Corporate and Institutional Digital.

How USC will work

There will be a USC for each currency currently traded globally. They will be convertible into cash at central banks, and exchanged in the same way as real-world currency – but more quickly.

Thibaut de Roux: This will reduce risk and costs

While dealings in many securities such as bonds and equities are already done digitally, clearing and settlement can take two to three days.

Thibaut de Roux, our Global Head of Markets, welcomed the development. “Introducing a digital cash alternative with many of the properties of central bank money means that the transfer of ownership of financial assets could be done immediately,” he said.

“This will reduce risk for the client, allow them to better manage liquidity, lower capital requirements, and ultimately reduce costs.”

Distributed ledgers

Distributed ledgers are revolutionising the way we do business. Not only in the capital markets but in the world of trade too.

Kaushalya Somasundaram: USC needs to be widely adopted

“In order to deliver the potential benefits promised by distributed ledger technologies in capital markets, the creation of real cash on a distributed ledger is key,” said Kaushalya Somasundaram, our Head of Fintech Strategy and Partnerships.

“Such a form of cash will need to conform to regulatory requirements and be recognised and adopted widely by the markets. The USC project sets out to achieve this.”

The USC project was started in 2015 by Swiss bank UBS and fintech firm Clearmatics Technologies.

HSBC joins nine other banks in the project – Banco Santander, Bank of New York Mellon, Deutsche Bank, NEX, Barclays, CIBC, Credit Suisse, MUFG and State Street.

How does USC differ from the digital currency bitcoin?

  • USC is a digital cash equivalent of real currencies, fully convertible at central banks
  • It will trade over a “permissioned” distributed ledger, open only to participating institutions
  • This is in contrast to bitcoin, which is non-domiciled, not backed by any real currency, and trades on an “open” blockchain accessible by all.
